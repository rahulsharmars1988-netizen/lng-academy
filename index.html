What Comes Out of the Ground

When natural gas is first extracted, it is rarely the clean product that reaches homes and industries. Raw gas is a complex mixture dominated by methane (CH₄) — typically 70–90% by volume — but it always arrives at the surface with unwanted companions.

These include heavier hydrocarbons such as ethane, propane, and butane — collectively called Natural Gas Liquids (NGLs). It also contains acid gases like carbon dioxide (CO₂) and hydrogen sulphide (H₂S), water vapour, and trace amounts of mercury. Each of these must be removed before the gas can be sold or exported.

Why does mercury matter? Even parts-per-billion concentrations of mercury will corrode the aluminium heat exchangers inside a liquefaction plant — causing catastrophic equipment failure. Mercury removal is therefore a mandatory first step in any gas processing facility destined to produce LNG.

The NGLs — propane, butane, ethane — are not waste products. Separated and stabilised, they become LPG for cooking, ethane feedstock for petrochemicals, or condensate for blending with crude oil. A well-designed processing plant captures these byproducts and converts them into additional revenue streams.

Two Types of Gas Reservoir

Associated gas is produced in the same reservoir as crude oil — it emerges alongside the oil whether the operator wants it or not. Historically, when no pipeline or processing infrastructure existed, this gas was simply flared (burned off at the wellhead). Nigeria's Niger Delta became one of the world's worst flaring sites, wasting billions of dollars in energy value and releasing massive amounts of CO₂.

Non-associated gas comes from reservoirs where gas is the primary product — no oil in significant quantities. The giant offshore fields of Mozambique, Tanzania, and Qatar are predominantly non-associated gas. The operator drills specifically to produce gas, and the economics must justify the investment purely on gas revenues.

Policy implication: For African governments with significant associated gas volumes — particularly Nigeria and Angola — developing gas gathering and processing infrastructure converts a flaring liability into an economic asset. This is often the lowest-cost path to building a domestic gas supply base.

What Gas Is Used For Domestically

Once processed, natural gas serves multiple sectors of an economy. The sequence of priorities typically follows the available infrastructure and credit quality of the buyer:

Power generation is almost always the first priority — electricity is the enabling infrastructure for all other economic activity. Gas-fired power plants can be permitted and constructed in 3–4 years, gas turbines are a proven technology, and the utility company typically has sufficient credit to backstop project financing through a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA).

Beyond power, gas serves as feedstock for fertiliser plants (converting methane to ammonia and urea), methanol plants, and petrochemical facilities. These industries require large, steady gas volumes and offer the opportunity to create manufacturing employment rather than simply exporting raw molecules.

Why −162°C?

Methane has a boiling point of −161.5°C at atmospheric pressure. Cool it below this temperature and it transitions from gas to liquid — a process driven by removing enough thermal energy that intermolecular van der Waals forces overcome molecular kinetic energy, pulling the molecules into a liquid state.

The practical result is extraordinary: LNG occupies just 1/600th the volume of the same mass of natural gas. A single large LNG carrier can hold the equivalent of approximately 6 billion cubic feet of gas — enough to meet the UK's total daily gas demand. Without this compression, maritime gas transport at scale would be physically impossible.

The engineering challenge: Maintaining a liquid at −162°C requires double-hulled insulated storage tanks made from 9% nickel steel (which retains ductility at cryogenic temperatures) and continuous management of "boil-off gas" (BOG) — the small fraction of LNG that naturally evaporates. Modern carriers use BOG as fuel for their propulsion systems.

Liquefaction Process Technologies

Three main refrigeration technology families dominate global LNG production. The choice affects capital cost, energy efficiency, physical footprint, and suitability for offshore deployment.

APCI C3MR (Air Products Propane Pre-cooled Mixed Refrigerant) is the most widely deployed technology globally — used in Qatar, Australia, Nigeria, Malaysia, and dozens of other projects. It pre-cools the gas using a propane refrigerant loop, then completes liquefaction using a mixed refrigerant of methane, ethane, propane, and nitrogen. The propane stage is highly efficient but creates a safety consideration — propane is heavier than air and accumulates in low-lying areas if leaked, creating explosion risk.

ConocoPhillips Cascade uses three separate pure refrigerant loops in sequence: propane cools to approximately −35°C, ethylene takes it to −90°C, and methane completes the liquefaction to −155°C. Each loop is simpler to control than a mixed refrigerant system. The process powers the US export facilities at Sabine Pass and Freeport LNG.

Shell DMR (Dual Mixed Refrigerant) uses two separate mixed refrigerant circuits — ideal for the rolling motion environment of a floating LNG (FLNG) vessel where propane storage creates additional risk. Shell's Prelude FLNG uses this approach.

Train Architecture and Why It Matters

An LNG plant is not built as a single monolithic unit. It is constructed as a series of parallel processing lines called "trains." Each train is an independent, complete liquefaction unit capable of operating on its own.

A typical world-scale plant has 2 to 7 trains, each producing 3.5 to 7.8 MTPA of LNG. The benefits of multi-train design are significant: if one train trips offline for maintenance or a safety event, the others continue producing. Expansion means adding a new train, not rebuilding the existing plant. Financing can be staged across train commissioning dates.

Qatar's Ras Laffan complex — the world's largest LNG production hub — has fourteen trains across the Qatargas and RasGas projects, producing approximately 77 MTPA total.

Eight Links, One Chain

The LNG value chain stretches from underground reservoirs to the end-user's burner. It comprises eight distinct links, each requiring separate investment, expertise, and contractual frameworks. The chain only functions when all eight links operate reliably and in coordination.

This interdependence creates a fundamental commercial principle: the value chain is only as strong as its weakest link. A regasification terminal not ready on time forces the first LNG cargo to be diverted to the spot market at a discount. A gas field underperforming its committed supply rates leaves a liquefaction plant running below capacity, threatening debt service ratios.

Historical example — West Africa Gas Pipeline (WAGP): A $590 million pipeline was built connecting Nigerian gas to Ghana, Benin, and Togo. The pipeline infrastructure itself worked. But the upstream gas supply link — disrupted by Niger Delta militancy and oil field operational issues — repeatedly failed to deliver committed volumes. The result: minimal revenues from a major infrastructure investment for years. One broken link broke the whole chain.

How LNG Gets from Liquefaction to Market

Once liquefied, LNG is loaded onto purpose-built carriers — the most technically complex commercial ships in the world. Every component that touches the cargo must operate reliably at −162°C while the ship rolls through ocean swells. The engineering challenge is maintaining cryogenic conditions at sea, 24 hours a day, for a voyage of days to weeks.

LNG carrier sizes evolved significantly over decades. The 1980s standard was approximately 125,000 cubic metres (m³) of cargo capacity. Modern carriers typically range from 160,000 to 180,000 m³. Qatar commissioned the world's largest class — Q-Max at 266,000 m³ and Q-Flex at 216,000 m³ — optimised for the long-haul Qatar-to-Asia route. These vessels are too large for the Panama Canal.

Containment Systems

Two main containment technologies compete in the LNG carrier market. Moss Rosenberg uses large independent spherical aluminium tanks that protrude above the deck — highly visible, self-supporting, and robust, but limiting cargo capacity relative to hull size. GTT Membrane (Mark III or NO96 systems) uses thin corrugated stainless steel or invar membranes bonded to an insulated box structure integrated into the ship's hull — maximising cargo volume but requiring precise structural integrity. The global fleet shifted heavily toward membrane technology through the 2000s.

FSRU — The Game Changer for Emerging Markets

A Floating Storage and Regasification Unit (FSRU) is either a purpose-built or converted LNG carrier that stores LNG and regasifies it onboard, sending pipeline-quality gas ashore via a flexible riser or jetty connection. For emerging import markets, FSRUs transformed the economics of LNG access.

A permanent onshore import terminal takes 4–5 years to build and costs $500M–$1B+. An FSRU can be deployed in 12–18 months and costs $150–300M in total charter or purchase cost. It can be relocated if market conditions change. Ghana's deployment of the Golar Spirit FSRU at Tema to supply gas-fired power plants is a real-world example that helped address the country's power crisis.

How LNG is Priced

LNG pricing is not uniform globally — it varies by market, contract vintage, and indexation method. Three major reference pricing systems exist.

In Asia, the dominant method is oil-indexed pricing tied to the Japan Crude Cocktail (JCC) — the average import crude oil price into Japan. The formula is P = α × JCC + β, where α (the slope, typically 12–14%) represents the ratio of LNG to oil value, and β is a base constant adjustment.

In the US, gas-on-gas competition prevails — LNG export contracts from Sabine Pass and other US terminals are priced as Henry Hub + liquefaction fee, typically adding $2.50–3.50/MMBtu above Henry Hub spot price.

In Europe, pricing is increasingly tied to NBP (UK National Balancing Point) or TTF (Netherlands Title Transfer Facility) hub prices, with LNG competing against Russian pipeline gas.

Take-or-Pay — Why Banks Lend Billions

LNG projects cost $10–30 billion to build. Commercial banks will only lend at that scale if they can see a reliable, contractually guaranteed revenue stream that will service debt over 20+ years. Take-or-pay (ToP) contracts provide exactly this mechanism.

Under a ToP clause, the buyer commits to pay for a minimum quantity of LNG — typically 80–90% of Annual Contract Quantity (ACQ) — whether or not they physically lift the cargo. If they don't take the gas, they still pay. This transforms market uncertainty into contractual certainty, giving lenders the confidence to commit long-term capital.

Price Re-openers: Long-term SPAs include price review clauses (every 3–5 years) allowing either party to request renegotiation if market conditions have changed "materially." The 2014–2016 oil price collapse — when oil fell from $110/bbl to below $30/bbl — triggered widespread price re-opener discussions as buyers on oil-indexed contracts saw spot market prices collapse far below their long-term contract rates.

Three Core Commercial Models

Every LNG export project must answer a fundamental question: who owns the gas at each stage, who owns the liquefaction plant, and how does money flow from LNG buyer back to gas producer? Three established structures answer this question in different ways.

Integrated: The same entities own the upstream gas field and the LNG export plant. Revenue flows directly from LNG buyers to the integrated consortium. No separate gas transfer price needs to be negotiated. Qatar's Qatargas, Australia's North West Shelf, and Norway's Snøhvit are integrated projects.

Merchant: Separate entities own the upstream and the LNG plant. The plant company buys gas from the upstream under a long-term gas SPA, then sells LNG under LNG SPAs. This allows different investors (including the government NOC) to hold equity in the plant separately from the upstream. Nigeria LNG, Angola LNG, and Trinidad LNG Trains 1–3 follow this structure.

Tolling: The LNG plant provides liquefaction as a service. The gas owner retains title to the gas and LNG throughout. The plant earns a fixed monthly reservation tariff plus a variable cargo charge. The gas owner bears all commodity price and market risk. Freeport LNG, Cameron LNG, and Cove Point in the US use tolling structures.

The Government's Dual Role

Every gas-producing government simultaneously wears two hats: it is the resource steward — representing citizens' long-term interests in how the national resource is developed — and a potential commercial participant through its national oil company.

These roles can create tension. As resource steward, the government wants maximum domestic benefit, low domestic gas prices, and strong local content requirements. As a commercial participant, it wants bankable project economics and creditworthy partners. Balancing these objectives is the central challenge of gas sector governance.

Domestic Supply Obligation (DSO)

A DSO is a regulatory instrument requiring gas producers to allocate a minimum percentage of their production to the domestic market before any gas can be exported. The rationale is straightforward: a country's citizens should benefit from their own natural resources before those resources are shipped abroad.

Real examples: Nigeria requires all operators to meet a minimum domestic gas obligation for power generation. Israel reserves 60% of its offshore gas for domestic use. Egypt mandates 30% domestic allocation. Western Australia requires 15% of all gas produced in the state to remain onshore.

DSO Trade-offs: A well-designed DSO stimulates domestic market development and ensures energy security. A poorly designed DSO — particularly one setting domestic prices below production cost — discourages investment, causes gas shortages, and can make the entire development unfinanceable. Getting the domestic price mechanism right is as important as setting the percentage.

Gas Master Plan — A Country's GPS for Gas Development

A Gas Master Plan (GMP) is the overarching strategic framework that guides a government's entire approach to gas sector development. It answers: How much gas do we have? What should we do with it? Who gets it first? At what price? Through what infrastructure? Governed by what regulations?

Key GMP elements include: reserve evaluation, domestic supply-demand analysis, export capacity planning, infrastructure development roadmap, pricing policy framework, institutional and regulatory architecture, and identification of anchor projects — large investments that justify the capital infrastructure required.

LNG Project Development Phases

An LNG export project progresses through structured phases, each a decision gate: proceed or exit. Capital commitments escalate dramatically at each stage.

Screening & Evaluation (6–12 months, $0.5–1M): Is the resource size, location, and market opportunity sufficient to justify a world-scale project? Minimum viable threshold: approximately 8 TCF of proven reserves to support a single 5 MTPA train for 20 years.

Pre-FEED (6 months, $2–5M): Preliminary designs and initial cost estimates. Identifies major technical and commercial risks.

FEED (12–18 months, $40–80M): Detailed engineering sufficient to invite binding EPC bids. Produces cost estimates at ±15–25% accuracy. This is the point of major cost commitment before FID.

EPC Bidding & Selection (6–8 months): Only 6–7 contractors globally are qualified for world-scale LNG EPC work. Competitive bidding historically saves 10–20% vs. single-source negotiation.

Final Investment Decision (FID): The moment of full commitment. Requires: government approvals, enabling legislation, financed LNG SPAs, confirmed project finance from lenders. EPC contract is awarded simultaneously.

EPC Construction (4–6 years, $1,500–$2,500 per tonne of capacity): Peak workforce of 5,000–8,000 workers. First 16 months focused on engineering and procurement in home offices. After month 16, activity shifts to site.

Africa at a Crossroads

Sub-Saharan Africa faces a paradox: some of the world's largest gas reserves discovered in the 21st century sit in countries where hundreds of millions of people have no reliable electricity. Mozambique and Tanzania together have discovered over 250 trillion cubic feet (TCF) of offshore gas — enough to supply the entire EU for over 15 years. Yet domestic electricity access rates remain among the lowest globally.

The challenge is connecting these molecules to domestic demand while also generating the export revenue needed to finance their development in the first place. An LNG export project requires a world-scale gas field and creditworthy international buyers. A domestic gas market requires infrastructure, local buyers with credit, and a regulatory framework — none of which exists overnight.

The Gas-to-Power Opportunity

Power generation is the natural first domestic market for new gas discoveries in Africa. The demand is massive, the technology is proven, and the utility company (even if government-owned) can provide a PPA that lenders accept as a bankable offtake. The US Power Africa initiative estimated that achieving 30,000 MW of additional power generation in sub-Saharan Africa by 2030 would require approximately 5.5 BCF/day of additional gas supply.

The FSRU solution for Africa: Several West African nations — Ghana, Ivory Coast, Senegal, and Benin — have used FSRUs to rapidly establish LNG import terminals, enabling gas-fired power generation without waiting for large domestic gas field development. This is particularly relevant where domestic gas supply is unreliable or constrained by pipeline disruptions.

Ghana: power blackouts cost $2M/day. No domestic gas. Choose your energy strategy.
Ghana's domestic gas from Jubilee field is unreliable. Daily 12-hour blackouts are costing the economy $2M/day ($730M/year). You have $300M budget and need gas for power within 18 months.
A. Charter an FSRU
Deploy in 12–18 months, $150–250M, relocatable
B. Build Onshore Terminal
4–5 years to build, $600M+, permanent
C. Build Pipeline from Nigeria
Extend WAGP — 2–3 years, depends on Nigeria supply reliability
D. Build Coal Power Plants
Fuel independent, 3–4 years, $400M+, 40-year lock-in
✅ Best choice for this situation. Ghana actually did this — the Golar Spirit FSRU was deployed at Tema. At $2M/day in blackout losses, delaying 12 months costs $730M — more than the FSRU itself. The FSRU is the only solution that fits both the 18-month timeline and the $300M budget. It can be returned or upgraded to a permanent terminal once market volumes justify the larger investment. This is exactly the "FSRU as bridge" strategy.
⚠️ Wrong timeline and budget. An onshore terminal takes 4–5 years (Ghana loses another $3.65B in blackout costs during construction) and costs $600M+ (exceeds budget). The right solution for the immediate crisis — but the right long-term solution once FSRU proves out the market volumes.
⚠️ High dependency risk. Nigeria's WAGP supply has historically been unreliable due to upstream disruptions. Ghana has already experienced this pain. Building additional infrastructure that depends on the same unreliable supply source compounds the risk without solving the fundamental problem. A diversified import solution (FSRU) is more resilient.
⚠️ Technology lock-in risk. Coal plants take 3–4 years to build, missing the 18-month window. They also create a 40-year carbon-intensive infrastructure commitment that conflicts with climate commitments and ESG financing constraints. Gas remains Ghana's best bridge fuel given its domestic resources.
